# MODULE 1 — Devanāgarī + Pronunciation + Sound System

(With English + IPA)

You already know Devanagari (Hindi), so this will be fast but complete. Sanskrit uses the same script but with **full phonetic precision**, no schwa-deletion like Hindi.

## 1. Sanskrit Vowels (Svarāḥ)

Sanskrit has **13 vowels**. All are pronounced exactly as written.

### **Short vowels**

1. **अ** – a – /ə/  
    Pure short "uh" sound.
    
2. **इ** – i – /i/  
    Short “i” as in “bit”.
    
3. **उ** – u – /u/  
    Short “u” as in “put”.
    
4. **ऋ** – ṛ – /r̩/  
    A syllabic “r”, like the “r” in “Kr̩ṣṇa”.
    
5. **ऌ** – ḷ – /l̩/  
    Rare. Syllabic “l”.
    

### **Long vowels**

6. **आ** – ā – /aː/  
    Long “aa” as in “father”.
    
7. **ई** – ī – /iː/  
    Long “ee”.
    
8. **ऊ** – ū – /uː/  
    Long “oo”.
    
9. **ॠ** – ṝ – /r̩ː/  
    Long syllabic “r”.
    
10. **ॡ** – ḹ – /l̩ː/  
    Rare.
    

### **Diphthongs**

11. **ए** – e – /eː/  
    “ay” (long).
    
12. **ऐ** – ai – /ai/  
    “eye”.
    
13. **ओ** – o – /oː/  
    “oh”.
    
14. **औ** – au – /au/  
    “ow” (house).

# MODULE 2 — VIBHAKTIS (THE 8 CASES OF SANSKRIT)

Sanskrit nouns decline according to:

1. **Gender** (masculine, feminine, neuter)
    
2. **Number** (singular, dual, plural)
    
3. **Case** (8 vibhaktis)
    

Hindi has only traces of this. Sanskrit has the **full system**.

To keep it manageable, we will learn cases using the very common masculine noun:

### **रामः** – rāmaḥ – “Rama” – /raːməḥ/

---

# THE 8 CASES — Meaning + Function

I’ll break them down like this:

**Case number**  
**Name** – **Meaning/Function**  
**Example (with pronunciation)**

---

## **1. Nominative (प्रथमा — prathamā)**

**Subject of the sentence**  
“Who does the action?”

- **रामः** – rāmaḥ – /raːməḥ/  
    Rama (as subject)
    

Sentence:  
**रामः गच्छति।** – rāmaḥ gacchati – “Rama goes.”

---

## **2. Accusative (द्वितीया — dvitīyā)**

**Object of the verb**  
“Whom/What?”

- **रामम्** – rāmam – /raːməm/
    

Sentence:  
**अहं रामम् पश्यामि।**  
ahaṃ rāmam paśyāmi  
“I see Rama.”

---

## **3. Instrumental (तृतीया — tṛtīyā)**

**Means / with / by / using**  
“By means of whom/what?”

- **रामेण** – rāmeṇa – /raːmeɳə/
    

Sentence:  
**रामेण सह गच्छामि।**  
rāmeṇa saha gacchāmi  
“I go with Rama.”

---

## **4. Dative (चतुर्थी — caturthī)**

**To / for**  
“Recipient of an action.”

- **रामाय** – rāmāya – /raːmaːjə/
    

Sentence:  
**फलम् रामाय ददामि।**  
phalam rāmāya dadāmi  
“I give the fruit to Rama.”

---

## **5. Ablative (पञ्चमी — pañcamī)**

**From / away from**  
“Source or cause.”

- **रामात्** – rāmāt – /raːmaːt̪/
    

Sentence:  
**सः रामात् आगच्छति।**  
saḥ rāmāt āgacchati  
“He comes from Rama.”

---

## **6. Genitive (षष्ठी — ṣaṣṭhī)**

**Of / belonging to**  
Possessive case.

- **रामस्य** – rāmasya – /raːməsjə/
    

Sentence:  
**एतत् रामस्य पुस्तकं।**  
etat rāmasya pustakam  
“This is Rama’s book.”

---

## **7. Locative (सप्तमी — saptamī)**

**In / on / at**  
Location case.

- **रामे** – rāme – /raːmeː/
    

Sentence:  
**ग्रामे रामः अस्ति।**  
grāme rāmaḥ asti  
“Rama is in the village.”

---

## **8. Vocative (संबोधन — sambodhana)**

**Calling address**  
“O Rama!”

- **हे राम!** – he rāma – /heː raːmə/
    

Sentence:  
**हे राम, आगच्छ!**  
he rāma, āgaccha!  
“O Rama, come!”

# QUICK EXPLANATION OF HOW CASES WORK TOGETHER

A simple sentence in Sanskrit will often contain **three or four cases**.

Example:

**रामः वनात् नगरं रथेन गच्छति।**  
rāmaḥ vanāt nagaraṃ rathena gacchati.  
“Rama goes from the forest to the city by chariot.”

Breakdown:

- **रामः** (rāmaḥ) — Nominative (subject)
    
- **वनात्** (vanāt) — Ablative (from the forest)
    
- **नगरम्** (nagaram) — Accusative (object = destination)
    
- **रथेन** (rathena) — Instrumental (by/with chariot)
    

You can already see how Sanskrit packs **maximum meaning with minimum words**.

# MODULE 3 — VERBS (धातवः dhātavaḥ)

Sanskrit verbs come from **root forms** called **dhātu**.  
Examples:

- **गम्** – gam – “to go”
    
- **पठ्** – paṭh – “to read”
    
- **भू** – bhū – “to be / become”
    
- **कृ** – kṛ – “to do”
    

Every verb root is conjugated across:

1. **Ten tenses/moods** (called **Lakāras**)
    
2. **Three persons**
    
3. **Three numbers**
    
4. **Three voices**
    

Very systematic.

---

# THE VOICES (Very important)

Sanskrit has **3 voices**:

### 1. **Parasmaipada**

— active voice, action goes “to others”  
(Equivalent to normal active: “I do”, “he says”)

### 2. **Ātmanepada**

— middle voice, action affects the subject  
(Like “I wash myself”, “he enjoys” — but broader)

### 3. **Ubhayapada**

— verbs that can take both voices

Example:

- भज् (bhaj) = worship → mostly ātmanepada
    
- गम् (gam) = go → parasmaipada
    
- कृ (kṛ) = do → ubhayapada
    

You’ll build intuition soon.

# MODULE 4 — SANDHI (THE ART OF JOINING)

**Sandhi = Joining sounds at word boundaries.**  
When two sounds meet, Sanskrit changes them to maintain smooth pronunciation.

Just like in Hindi we say:

- "क्या **है**" → "क्या **है**" (no change)
    
- But "एक **आदमी**" → "एकादमी" (sounds join)
    

Sanskrit does this with **perfect mathematical rules**.

There are three kinds:

1. **Vowel Sandhi (स्वर सन्धि)**
    
2. **Consonant Sandhi (व्यञ्जन सन्धि)**
    
3. **Visarga Sandhi (विसर्ग सन्धि)**
    

We will handle them one by one.

---

# PART 1 — VOWEL SANDHI (स्वर-सन्धि)

When a word ending with a vowel meets a word starting with a vowel, **both vowels transform**.

We start with the three master rules:

---

## RULE 1 — गुण Sandhi (a + i = e, a + u = o)

**A vowel + another vowel → stronger vowel.**

### 1. a + i = e

Example:

- **राम + इति → रामेति**  
    rāma + iti → rāmēti  
    /raːmə + ɪt̪i/ → /raːmeːt̪i/  
    Meaning: “Rama said thus…”
    

### 2. a + ī = ai

Example:

- **कृष्ण + ईशः → कृष्णैशः**  
    kṛṣṇa + īśaḥ → kṛṣṇaiśaḥ  
    /kr̩ʂɳə + iːʂəḥ/ → /kr̩ʂɳaɪʂəḥ/
    

### 3. a + u = o

Example:

- **गुरु + उपदेशः → गुरोऽपदेशः**  
    guru + upadeśaḥ → goro’ṅpadeśaḥ  
    Pronounced: goropadeśaḥ  
    /gʊrʊ + upəd̪eːʂəḥ/ → /goːrəpəd̪eːʂəḥ/
    

### 4. a + ū = au

Example:

- **देव + ऊहः → देवौहः**  
    deva + ūhaḥ → devauhaḥ  
    /d̪eːʋə + uːhəḥ/ → /d̪eːʋaʊhəḥ/

## RULE 2 — वृद्धि Sandhi (ā + i = ai, ā + u = au)

Stronger than guṇa.

### 1. ā + i = ai

Example:

- **कथा + इति → कथैति**  
    kathā + iti → kathaiti  
    /kət̪ʰaː + ɪt̪i/ → /kət̪ʰaɪt̪i/
    

### 2. ā + u = au

Example:

- **विद्या + उपकारः → विद्यौपकारः**  
    vidyā + upakāraḥ → vidyaupakāraḥ  
    /ʋɪd̪jaː + upəkaːrəḥ/ → /ʋɪd̪jaʊpəkaːrəḥ/
    

---

## RULE 3 — SAVARNA DIRGHA SANDHI (same vowels → long vowel)

**If same vowels meet: they join and become long.**

Examples:

### 1. a + a = ā

- **तत + अपि → तदपि**  
    tat + api → tadapi  
    (final t becomes d due to consonant sandhi)  
    /t̪ət̪ + əpɪ/ → /t̪əd̪əpɪ/
    

### 2. i + i = ī

- **ति + इह → तीह**  
    ti + iha → tīha  
    /t̪ɪ + iːɦə/ → /t̪iːɦə/
    

### 3. u + u = ū

- **सु + उक्ति → सूक्ति**  
    su + ukti → sūkti  
    /sʊ + ʊkt̪i/ → /suːkt̪i/
    

Also works for **ṛ + ṛ = ṝ**.

# PART 2 — VISARGA SANDHI (ः changes)

Visarga (ः – ḥ) is extremely dynamic.

### RULE A — Before S/Ś/Ṣ → Visarga becomes **s** or **ṣ**

Example:

- **हरिः + शरणम् → हरिःशरणम् / हर्षरणम्**  
    hariḥ + śaraṇam → hariśśaraṇam → harṣaraṇam  
    /hərɪḥ ʃərəɳəm/ → /hərʂərəɳəm/
    

### RULE B — Before vowels → visarga becomes “r” or disappears

Example:

- **रामः + इति → राम इति / रामेरिति**  
    rāmaḥ + iti → rāma iti  
    /raːməḥ + ɪt̪i/ → /raːmə ɪt̪i/
    

Example:

- **गुरुः + एव → गुरुरेव**  
    guruḥ + eva → gurur eva  
    /gʊrʊḥ + eːʋə/ → /gʊrʊreːʋə/

# PART 3 — CONSONANT SANDHI (व्यञ्जन-सन्धि)

When one word ends in a consonant and the next begins with a consonant.

### RULE — Voice Assimilation

Voicing shifts to match the next consonant.

Examples:

### 1. Final “t” → “d” before voiced sounds

- **tat + bhavati → tad bhavati**  
    तत् + भवति → तद्भवति  
    /t̪ət̪ + bʱəʋət̪i/ → /t̪əd̪ bʱəʋət̪i/
    

### 2. Final “s” → “ṣ/ś/s” depending on next consonant

Example:

- **kas + cit → kaścit**  
    कः + चित् → कश्चित्  
    /kəh + t͡ɕɪt̪/ → /kəʃt͡ɕɪt̪/
    

### 3. Nasal assimilation (n → m before labials)

- **tad + bhavati → tadvh…** (becomes not used often)
    
- Better example:  
    **san + bhavati → sambhavati**  
    सन् + भवति → सम्भवति  
    /sən + bʱəʋət̪i/ → /səmbʱəʋət̪i/

## MODULE 5  
**DECLENSIONS — LEVEL 1: The First 3 Cases (Nominative, Accusative, Instrumental)**

### 1. What Are Declensions?

In Sanskrit, nouns change their form depending on their **case** (vibhakti) and **number** (singular, dual, plural).  
Hindi also has this, but Sanskrit is far more systematic.

Sanskrit has **8 cases**:

1. Prathamā (Nominative) – subject
    
2. Dvitīyā (Accusative) – object
    
3. Tṛtīyā (Instrumental) – by/with
    
4. Chaturthī (Dative) – for/to
    
5. Panchamī (Ablative) – from
    
6. Ṣaṣṭhī (Genitive) – of
    
7. Saptamī (Locative) – in/on/at
    
8. Sambodhana (Vocative) – addressing
    

Today we do **first three** only.

## 7. QUICK USAGE TIP

A Sanskrit sentence often has **3–4 different cases** in one line. Example:

**रामः गृहे पुस्तकं रामाय ददाति।**  
rāmaḥ gṛhe pustakaṃ rāmāya dadāti  
/Rāma gives the book to Rama in the house/

- रामः → nominative
    
- गृहे → locative
    
- पुस्तकं → accusative
    
- रामाय → dative
    

Sanskrit expresses **subject, object, place, and recipient** all in one sentence, with minimal words.

## 2. **COMPOUND WORDS (समास)**

Sanskrit loves **compounding**. Words join to form **long, precise words**.

### Basic Types of Samāsa:

1. **Dvandva (dvandva compound)** – “and”  
    Example: **रामः सीतया च गच्छति** → **राम-सीता** (Rāma and Sītā)
    
2. **Tatpurusha (dependent compound)** – “modifier + noun”  
    Example: **राजपुत्रः** – rāja-putra – “king’s son”
    
    - rāja = king, putra = son → “king’s son”  
        IPA: /ˈraːd͡ʒə put̪rə/
        
3. **Karmadhāraya (descriptive compound)** – “adjective + noun”  
    Example: **सुन्दरपुरम्** – sundara-puram – “beautiful city”  
    IPA: /sʊn̪d̪əra puːrəm/
    
4. **Bahuvrihi (possessive / external reference)** – “possessing X”  
    Example: **पितृभक्तः** – pitṛ-bhaktaḥ – “one devoted to father”  
    IPA: /pɪt̪r̩ bʱəkt̪əɦ/
    
5. **Digu / Avyayībhāva** – adverbs or direction compounds  
    Example: **उत्तरप्रदेश** – uttarapradeśa – “northern region”  
    IPA: /ʊt̪ːərəprəd̪eːʂə/
    

---

## 3. **HOW ADJECTIVES WORK IN COMPOUNDS**

- If adjective + noun are joined (Karmadhāraya), the **adjective usually loses final -a** in compounds:
    

Example:

- सुंदर + नगर = सुन्दरनगर (sundarnagara) – “beautiful city”
    
- IPA: /sʊn̪d̪ərnəɡərə/
    
- If adjective stands alone: **सुन्दर नगर (sundara nagara)** – same meaning, but separated.

# MODULE 8 — ADVANCED VERBS, MOODS, AND COMPOUNDS

Sanskrit verbs are extremely systematic. They **change for:**

1. **Person (पुरुष)** – 1st, 2nd, 3rd
    
2. **Number (वचन)** – Singular, Dual, Plural
    
3. **Tense / Mood (काल / लकार)** – Present, Imperfect, Future, Perfect, Conditional, etc.
    
4. **Voice (पुरुष / भक्ति)** – Active (Parasmaipada) / Middle (Atmanepada)
    

We’ll proceed step by step.

---

## 1. PRESENT TENSE (लट्) — ACTIVE

Example verb: **गम् (gam)** – “to go”  
IPA: /ɡəm/  
Pronunciation: **gam**

### 1st Person Singular:

**अहं गच्छामि।**  
ahaṃ gacchāmi  
/əɦəŋ ɡət͡ʃːaːmi/  
“I go”

### 2nd Person Singular:

**त्वं गच्छसि।**  
tvaṃ gacchasi  
/t̪ʋəŋ ɡət͡ʃːəsɪ/  
“You go”

### 3rd Person Singular:

**सः गच्छति।**  
saḥ gacchati  
/səɦ ɡət͡ʃːət̪i/  
“He goes”

---

### 1st Person Dual:

**आवम् गच्छावः।**  
āvam gacchāvaḥ  
/ɑːʋəm ɡət͡ʃːaːʋəɦ/  
“We two go”

### 3rd Person Dual:

**ते गच्छतः।**  
te gacchataḥ  
/te ɡət͡ʃːət̪əɦ/  
“They two go”

### 1st Person Plural:

**वयम् गच्छामः।**  
vayam gacchāmaḥ  
/ʋəjəm ɡət͡ʃːaːməɦ/  
“We go”

### 3rd Person Plural:

**ते गच्छन्ति।**  
te gacchanti  
/te ɡət͡ʃːən̪t̪i/  
“They go”

> **TIP:** Dual forms are almost identical to 1st/3rd plural endings. Hindi has no dual, but Sanskrit keeps it distinct.

## 2. FUTURE TENSE (लृट् / लट् प्रत्यय + भविष्यत्)

**Verb:** गम् → गमिष्यति  
IPA: /ɡəmiʃjət̪i/  
Pronunciation: **gamiṣyati**  
“He will go”

|---|---|---|---|
|1sg|गमिष्यामि|gacmiṣyāmi|I will go|
|2sg|गमिष्यसि|gacmiṣyasi|You will go|
|3sg|गमिष्यति|gacmiṣyati|He will go|

## 3. IMPERFECT / PAST (लङ्)

    “He went”

## 4. CONDITIONAL / OPTATIVE (विधिलिङ्)

Used to express: “would go / might go”

- Singular: **सः गन्तुम् इच्छेत्।**  
    saḥ gantum icchet  
    /səɦ ɡənt̪um ɪt͡ʃːet̪/  
    “He might go”

## 5. IMPERATIVE (लोट् / आज्ञा)

Used to **command or request**.

- Singular: **गच्छ!** – gaccha! – /ɡət͡ʃːə/ – “Go!”
    
- Dual: **गच्छताम्!** – gacchatām – /ɡət͡ʃːət̪aːm/ – “You two go!”
    
- Plural: **गच्छन्तु!** – gacchantu – /ɡət͡ʃːən̪t̪u/ – “You all go!”
    

---

## 6. ADVANCED COMPOUND VERBS (समास क्रिया)

Sanskrit can **merge verbs + objects / prepositions** to make **single-word verbs**.

### Examples:

1. **गृहं आगच्छति → गृहमागच्छति**  
    gṛhaṃ āgacchati → gṛham āgacchati  
    /ɡrəɦəŋ aːɡət͡ʃːət̪i/  
    “He comes to the house”
    
2. **फलं खादति → फलखादति**  
    phalaṃ khādati → phalakādati  
    /ˈpʰələm kʰaːd̪ət̪i/  
    “He eats fruit”
    

> Sanskrit prefers **single, precise words** over multiple prepositions + verb. This is why it’s called a “mathematical language.”

# MODULE 9 — POETRY, METERS (CHANDAS), AND ADVANCED SENTENCES

Sanskrit poetry is built on **syllabic patterns**, rhythm, and precise grammar. It is highly structured.

---

## 1. **CHANDAS (METER)**

A **chandas** is a rhythmic pattern based on **syllables**.

Sanskrit syllables are classified into:

1. **Laghu (light)** – short vowel
    
    - Example: अ /a/, इ /i/, उ /u/
        
    - IPA: /ə/, /i/, /u/
        
2. **Guru (heavy)** – long vowel or syllable ending with a consonant
    
    - Example: आ /ā/, ई /ī/, ऊ /ū/, अः /aḥ/
        
    - IPA: /aː/, /iː/, /uː/, /əɦ/
        

---

### Common Meters

1. **Anushtubh (अनुष्टुभ्)**
    

- 4 padas (lines) × 8 syllables = 32 syllables
    
- Most common, used in Mahabharata & Ramayana
    
- Example:  
    **रामो विग्रहवान् वीरः गच्छति वनम्।**  
    rāmo vigrahavān vīraḥ gacchati vanam  
    /raːmo ʋɪɡrəɦəʋɑːn ʋiːrəɦ ɡət͡ʃːət̪i ʋənəm/  
    “Rāma, strong and brave, goes to the forest.”
    

2. **Shloka (श्लोक)** – mostly Anushtubh
    

- Two halves of 16 syllables each
    
- Can rhyme and follow more elaborate patterns
    

3. **Tristubh (त्रिष्टुभ्)**
    

- 11 syllables per line × 4 lines = 44 syllables
    
- Often used in Rigveda hymns
    

4. **Jagati (जगती)**
    

- 12 syllables × 4 lines = 48 syllables
    
- Used in classical Sanskrit poetry for longer, majestic lines
    

---

## 2. **POETIC DEVICES (ALANKARAS / अलंकार)**

Sanskrit poetry uses **figurative language**, similar to English but far more structured.

|Device|Sanskrit|Meaning|Example|
|---|---|---|---|
|Upama|उपमा|Simile|रामः सिंहवत् वीरः। (Rāma is brave **like a lion**)|
|Rupaka|रूपक|Metaphor|रामः सिंहः। (Rāma is a lion)|
|Anuprasa|अनुप्रास|Alliteration|सीता संसेविता सुशिक्षिता। (Sīta served, carefully trained – repeated 's')|
|Yamaka|यमक|Rhyming|गीतं सीतया गेयम्। (poem sung by Sīta)|

---

## 3. **ADVANCED SENTENCE CONSTRUCTION**

Sanskrit allows **multiple ideas in one sentence** using **compound words (Samāsa)**.

### Example:

**Rāma, the brave son of the king, will go to the beautiful forest tomorrow.**

Sanskrit:  
**राजपुत्रः शूरो रामः सुन्दरं वनं श्वः गमिष्यति।**  
rājaputraḥ śūro rāmaḥ sundaraṃ vanaṃ śvaḥ gamiṣyati  
/raːd͡ʒəput̪rəɦ ʃuːrəɦ raːməɦ sʊn̪d̪ərəŋ ʋənəŋ ʃʋəɦ ɡəmiʃjət̪i/

- राजपुत्रः शूरः → brave son of the king
    
- सुन्दरं वनं → beautiful forest
    
- श्वः गमिष्यति → will go tomorrow
    

> Notice: Sanskrit conveys **subject, attributes, object, time, and action** with precision.

---

## 4. **LONG COMPOUND EXAMPLE (Bahuvrihi / Tatpurusha)**

- **रामपुत्रशूरवीरगमनाय तत्पूर्वं वनम् आगच्छति।**  
    rāmaputra-śūra-vīra-gamanāya tatpūrvaṃ vanaṃ āgacchati  
    /raːməput̪rə ʃuːrə ʋiːrə ɡəmənaːjə tətpʊːrʋəŋ ʋənəŋ aːɡət͡ʃːət̪i/  
    “Rāma, the brave son of the king, goes to the forest as prepared.”
    

Here:

- **Rāmaputra-śūra-vīra** → Tatpurusha compound
    
- **Gamanāya** → gerundive form, “for going”
    
- Sanskrit allows packing **entire clauses into single words or compounds**.
