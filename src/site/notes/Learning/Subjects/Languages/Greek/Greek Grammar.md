# **1. Two Verb Groups in Greek**

Every Modern Greek verb ends in either:

### **A. -ω verbs (most common)**

Example: **γράφω** (I write)

### **B. -μαι verbs (reflexive/passive pattern)**

These endings attach to the verb stem.

|Person|Greek Ending|Example Meaning|
|---|---|---|

|he/she/it|-ει|he writes|
|we|-ουμε|we write|
|you (pl)|-ετε|you (pl) write|
|they|-ουν(ε)|they write|

# **MODULE 4 — Greek Past Tenses**

# **1. Overview of Greek Past Tenses**

Greek has **three main past tenses**:

1. **Aorist** → simple past  
    “I wrote / I saw / I ate (once).”
    
2. **Imperfect** → past continuous  
    “I was writing / I used to write.”
    
3. **Perfect** → resultative past  
    “I have written / I have seen.”
    

The first big tool you must know is the **augment**.

---

# **2. The Augment (Ε-)**

Most past tense verbs in Greek add an **ε-** at the front:

γράφω → **έγραψα** (I wrote)  
βλέπω → **είδα** (I saw) – irregular  
τρέχω → **έτρεξα** (I ran)

If the verb starts with a vowel, it **lengthens**:

α → η  
ε → η  
ο → ω  
ι → ι (unchanged)  
υ → υ (unchanged)

Example:  
αγαπάω → **αγάπησα** (I loved)

# **5. Aorist vs Imperfect (Simple Rule)**

### **Aorist = one-time, completed action**

I wrote the letter → **Έγραψα το γράμμα.**

### **Imperfect = ongoing or repeated action**

I was writing a letter → **Έγραφα ένα γράμμα.**

Think of Aorist as **Past Simple**, Imperfect as **Past Continuous**.

---

# **6. Perfect (Have done)**

Formed with:

**έχω + past participle**

Examples:

1. **Έχω γράψει.**  
    I have written.  
    ekho GRA-psee – /ˈexo ˈɣrapsi/
    
2. **Έχεις δει;**  
    Have you seen?  
    E-khis thee – /ˈeçis ði/
    
3. **Έχουμε πάει.**  
    We have gone.
    

This tense is simpler than English because Greek participles rarely change.

# **8. How to Instantly Identify Case in Any Sentence**

1. **Look at the article first.**  
    ο / η / το → nominative  
    τον / την / το → accusative  
    του / της / του → genitive
    
2. **Check the verb.**  
    If the noun receives the action → accusative  
    If the noun owns something → genitive
    
3. **If you’re talking _to_ someone** → vocative
    
4. **Neuter nouns rarely change**, so trust the article.

# **MODULE 7 — Syntax & Sentence Building**

Greek syntax is flexible but **not chaotic**.  
It follows a very stable logic:

1. **Basic word order: S – V – O**
    
2. **But pronouns, articles, cases, and verb endings allow variations.**
    
3. **Meaning comes from endings**, not position.

# **1. Basic Greek Word Order (SVO)**

### Standard neutral sentence:

**Ο Γιάννης τρώει ένα μήλο.**  
**/o ˈʝanis ˈtroi ˈena ˈmilo/**  
Giannis eats an apple.

- Ο Γιάννης = subject
    
- τρώει = verb
    
- ένα μήλο = object
    

---

# **2. When Greek Moves Words Around (and why)**

Because Greek uses **cases**, it can move elements for **emphasis**.

### Emphasis on the object:

**Ένα μήλο τρώει ο Γιάννης.**  
**/ˈena ˈmilo ˈtroi o ˈʝanis/**  
It's an apple that Giannis is eating.

### Emphasis on the verb:

**Τρώει ο Γιάννης ένα μήλο.**  
Focus is on the _action_.

### Emphasis on the subject:

**Ο Γιάννης τρώει ένα μήλο.**  
Neutral → subject focus.

**Meaning stays mostly the same.**  
Only Emphasis changes.

---

# **3. The Most Important Rule of Greek Syntax**

## **The verb almost always stays in 2nd position**, especially in clear sentences.

Even when you shuffle the sentence, you usually keep:

**[Focus] + [Verb] + [Rest]**

Example:  
**Τώρα διαβάζω το βιβλίο.**  
**/ˈtora ðiaˈvazo to viˈvlio/**  
Now I read the book.

# **2. Greek Voices (Active / Middle / Passive)**

Greek uses **one verb**, but it has two sets of endings:

1. **Active** — πράττω (I act)
    
2. **Middle/Passive** — πράττομαι (I act for myself / I am acted upon)
    

Example:

- γράφω (I write)
    
- γράφομαι (I register myself)

# **7. Simple Past (Aorist)**

The hardest part of Greek verbs.  
Once you learn this, the whole language becomes easy.

---

## **A. Aorist Active Formula**

**έ + stem + suffix (-σα, -ες, -ε…)**

Example: γράφω → έγραψα /ˈeɣrapsa/  
Example: μαθαίνω → έμαθα  
Example: παίζω → έπαιξα

# **8. Simple Future (Active)**

Structure:  
**θα + present form**

Example:  
θα γράψω — I will write  
θα παίξω — I will play  
θα αγαπήσω — I will love

This future uses the **aorist stem**:

- γράψω
    
- παίξω
    
- αγαπήσω
    

---

# **9. Continuous Future**

Structure:  
**θα + present tense**

Examples:

- θα γράφω  
    I will be writing
    
- θα παίζω  
    I will be playing
    

---

# **10. Perfect System**

Greek forms perfect using **έχω + participle**.

### Present Perfect

**έχω γράψει**  
I have written

### Past Perfect

**είχα γράψει**  
I had written

### Future Perfect

**θα έχω γράψει**  
I will have written

These forms are extremely stable.

---

# **11. Passive Voice**

### Present Passive

γράφ-ομαι (I am written)

|Person|Form|
|---|---|
|1sg|γράφομαι|
|2sg|γράφεσαι|
|3sg|γράφεται|
|1pl|γραφόμαστε|
|2pl|γράφεστε|
|3pl|γράφονται|

---

### Past Passive (Aorist Passive)

Suffix: **-θηκα**

Example:

γράφω → γράφτηκα  
I was written / I got enrolled

Other examples:

αγαπώ → αγαπήθηκα  
I was loved

παίζω → παίχτηκα  
I was played

# **12. Full Conjugation Summary Table**

## **Tenses (Active):**

- Present → γράφω
    
- Imperfect → έγραφα
    
- Aorist → έγραψα
    
- Continuous Future → θα γράφω
    
- Simple Future → θα γράψω
    
- Present Perfect → έχω γράψει
    
- Past Perfect → είχα γράψει
    
- Future Perfect → θα έχω γράψει
    

## **Tenses (Passive):**

- Present → γράφομαι
    
- Imperfect → γραφόμουν
    
- Aorist → γράφτηκα
    
- Simple Future Passive → θα γραφτώ
    
- Aorist Future Passive → θα γράφτηκα (rare)
    
- Perfect → έχω γραφτεί

# **13. How to Conjugate ANY Greek Verb (Master Formula)**

1. **Find the stem**  
    Remove the ending:  
    γράφ-ω → γράφ  
    αγαπ-ώ → αγαπ  
    παιζ-ω → παιζ
    
2. **Find the aorist stem**  
    Look up or learn patterns:
    

- γράφ → γράψ
    
- παίζ → παίξ
    
- βάζ → βάλ
    
- βλέπ → είδ
    

3. **Add endings**  
    Greek endings are extremely stable.
    
4. **Use “θα” for the future**  
    θα + present  
    θα + aorist
    
5. **Use έχω + participle for perfect**
