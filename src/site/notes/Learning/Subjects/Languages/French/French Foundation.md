# FOUNDATIONS — Full tutorial

This lesson covers the absolute basics you need to read, write and speak French with confidence: the alphabet and accents, pronunciation building blocks (syllables, liaison, elision, silent letters), punctuation & capitalization conventions, and the pragmatic foundation of **tu / vous**. I keep things precise and practical — lots of examples and quick rules you can apply immediately.

---

# Alphabet, accents, and pronunciation basics

### Alphabet

French uses the 26-letter Latin alphabet (a → z). Speak each letter roughly as in these examples:

- a /a/, b /be/, c /se/, d /de/, e /ə/ or /ø/ (name: «e»), f /ɛf/, g /ʒe/, h /aʃ/, i /i/, j /ʒi/, k /ka/, l /ɛl/, m /ɛm/, n /ɛn/, o /o/, p /pe/, q /ky/, r /ɛʁ/, s /ɛs/, t /te/, u /y/, v /ve/, w /dubləve/, x /iks/, y /igʁɛk/, z /zɛd/.
    

### Accents — what they do and why they matter

Only vowels take accents. Each accent changes pronunciation or distinguishes words.

- **L’accent aigu (é)** — only on _e_ (é). Pronounced /e/ (close mid front). Example: _été_ /ete/ (summer).
    
- **L’accent grave (è, à, ù)** — on _e_ gives /ɛ/ (open e): _père_ /pɛʁ/. On _à, ù_ it usually distinguishes words (ex. _à_ = to; _a_ = has; _où_ vs _ou_).
    
- **L’accent circonflexe (â, ê, î, ô, û)** — often marks historical loss of an /s/ (hôpital ← hospital). It can affect vowel quality (ê ≈ /ɛ/) and sometimes distinguishes words: _sur_ vs _sûr_.
    
- **La cédille (ç)** — under _c_ to produce /s/ before a, o, u: _garçon_ /gaʁsɔ̃/.
    
- **Le tréma (ë, ï, ü)** — indicates the vowel is pronounced separately (diphthong separation): _naïf_ /na.if/, _Noël_ /nɔ.ɛl/.
    

### Pronunciation essentials

- French is **syllable-timed** (even rhythm) and word stress falls weakly on the **final syllable of a word group** (not like heavy English stress).
    
- Vowel quality matters — learn the distinct sounds for _e, é, è, eu, œ, u_.
    
- The French _r_ is a uvular sound /ʁ/ (back of the throat) in standard Parisian French.

# Syllables, liaison, elision, and silent letters

### Syllables — how to split and where stress sits

- A syllable in French is built around one vowel sound. Typical pattern: (consonants) + vowel (+ consonant).
    
- Stress: give a slight emphasis to the **final syllable** of a prosodic group (word or phrase), not to every word the way in English:
    
    - _je parle français_ → slight stress on _fran-çais_.
        

### Liaison — linking consonant sounds

**Liaison** = pronounce a normally silent final consonant to link to the next word that begins with a vowel sound.

- **Obligatory liaison** (must be pronounced):
    
    - between subject pronoun and verb (nous avons /nu.za.vɔ̃/), after determiners (les amis /le.z‿ami/), after adjectives before noun (petits enfants /pə.ti.z‿ɑ̃fɑ̃/).
        
- **Optional liaison** (contextual; more formal or careful speech):
    
    - after plural nouns in some fixed expressions, after some adverbs.
        
- **Forbidden liaison** (don’t do it):
    
    - after singular noun, after _et_ (never _et-alors_), before an aspirated _h_ (see below).
        

Examples:

- _Ils ont_ → /il.z‿ɔ̃/ (liaison: s → z).
    
- _Vous avez_ → /vu.z‿ave/ (vowel linking).
    

**Practical rule:** learn the common obligatory liaisons first (pronoun+verb, determiner+noun). In unsure situations, avoid a risky liaison in informal speech.

### Elision — dropping a vowel + apostrophe

When a word ending in an unstressed vowel (_e, a, i_ etc.) meets a following word starting with a vowel or mute h, French drops the vowel and writes an apostrophe:

- **je → j’** before vowel: _j’aime_ (not _je aime_).
    
- **le / la → l’**: _l’homme_ (not _le homme_).
    
- **ne → n’**: _n’est pas_.
    

**Note:** Some words do not elide (e.g., _et_ does not elide: _et elle_ remains).

### Silent letters — common patterns

Many final letters are not pronounced. Learn patterns rather than individual letters.

- **Very commonly silent:** final _e_ (called _e muet_), final _s, x, z_ in many words: _fille_ /fij/ (final e silent), _les_ /le/ (s silent), _deux_ /dø/ (x silent).
    
- **Often pronounced at the end:** _r, f, c, l_ are frequently pronounced (but check each word): _par_ /paʁ/, _chef_ /ʃɛf/, _avec_ /avɛk/, _bal_ /bal/.
    
- **Exceptions & liaison:** a normally silent final consonant may be pronounced in liaison (e.g., _ils_ pronounced /il/ alone, but /il.z‿ont/ with liaison).
    

**Practical rule:** if you’re unsure, learn word pronunciations from native audio/dictionary; focus on final _e_ and plural _s_ which are usually silent.

# Capitalization and punctuation rules

### Capitalization

- **Days, months, languages, nationalities**: **lowercase** in French.
    
    - _lundi, mars, français, anglais_.
        
- **Proper names**: capitalized (_Paris, Marie, Emmanuel Macron_).
    
- **Titles**: only the first word and proper nouns normally capitalized in running text: _Le seigneur des anneaux_ (but formal titles may vary).
    
- **Formal address**: _Monsieur, Madame_ capitalized in salutations.
    

### Punctuation — spaces and marks

French typography differs slightly from English.

- **Space before** the following punctuation marks: **; : ? !**
    
    - Correct: _Bonjour ; comment ça va ?_ (In typing, use a non-breaking space in print.)
        
- **No space before** comma and period: _Bonjour, je m’appelle…_
    
- **Quotation marks:** French guillemets « … » are standard with thin spaces inside: « Bonjour ». In digital text, you may use "..." but guillemets are formal.
    
- **Hyphenation in compound verbs and numbers:** hyphens are used in verbs formed with _t-on_ (e.g., _Sait-il ?_) and in modern spelled numbers (e.g., _vingt-et-un_). (Orthographic reforms slightly changed rules in 1990; follow current style guides for formal writing.)
    
- **Ellipsis:** three dots “…” — same usage as English.
    
- **Capital after colon:** usually **no** capital unless a full sentence follows.
    

### Quotation and direct speech

- Direct speech is set off by « … » or by dash in dialogues:
    
    - « Je viens, dit-il. »

# Informal vs formal speech (tu vs vous) — practical guidance

### Basic social rule

- **tu** = singular familiar (friends, family, children, peers when invited).
    
- **vous** = plural or polite singular (formal situations, strangers, elders, professional contexts).
    

### Morphology & verb forms

- _tu_ uses **2nd person singular** verb forms: _tu parles, tu vas, tu as_.
    
- _vous_ uses **2nd person plural** forms: _vous parlez, vous allez, vous avez_.
    
- The verb forms differ, so choosing _tu/vous_ changes conjugation.
    

### Pronouns and possessives

- Possessive adjectives change with the possessor AND the noun:
    
    - _ton / ta / tes_ (your — tu)
        
    - _votre / vos_ (your — vous)
        
- Using _vous_ signals distance/respect; _tu_ signals intimacy.
    

### Pragmatics: when to use which

- Use **vous**:
    
    - in professional emails/meetings,
        
    - with elders or unknown adults,
        
    - when you want to be polite or cautious.
        
- Use **tu**:
    
    - when invited to do so,
        
    - among children, close friends, family,
        
    - in many online communities and youthful contexts.
        

### Politeness formulas

- Formal: _Bonjour, Monsieur/Madame. Pourriez-vous me dire… ?_
    
- Informal: _Salut ! Tu peux me dire… ?_
    

**Switching registers:** if someone uses _tu_ with you, you may switch to _tu_ back. If you want to propose _tu_, polite formula: _On peut se tutoyer ? / Tu veux qu’on se tutoie ?_

# Quick practice exercises (with short answers)

1. Write the formal equivalent of: _Tu peux m’aider ?_  
    **Answer:** _Pouvez-vous m’aider ?_
    
2. Identify the liaison and whether it’s obligatory: _nous avons_ → pronounce?  
    **Answer:** Liaison /nu.za.vɔ̃/ — **obligatory**.
    
3. Elide correctly: _Je ai/ Je ne ai_ → ?  
    **Answer:** _J’ai / Je n’ai_.
    
4. Accent identification: Which accent changes _ou_ (or) vs _où_ (where)?  
    **Answer:** _où_ has **accent grave** to distinguish from _ou_.
    
5. Capitalize correctly: _je pars en june, lundi_ → ?  
    **Answer:** _Je pars en juin, lundi._ (months/days lowercase)

# Practical tips to internalize the foundations

1. **Listen and mimic.** Pronunciation, liaison, elision — all become natural through listening (news, podcasts, songs).
    
2. **Learn accents with words, not in isolation.** e.g., _été, père, forêt, garçon, naïf_ — seeing them in context helps.
    
3. **Practice obligatory liaisons first.** Pronoun+verb, determiner+noun — those will immediately boost your fluency.
    
4. **When in doubt about liaison, be conservative in informal speech.** In formal speech choose the liaison if you’re comfortable.
    
5. **Use _vous_ by default until invited otherwise.** It’s safer socially.
    
6. **Read aloud.** That trains syllable timing and final-syllable stress.
