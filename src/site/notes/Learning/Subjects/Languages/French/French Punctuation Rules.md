## **1. Period / Full Stop (.)**

- Used at the end of declarative sentences, just like in English.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - Il fait beau aujourd’hui. → It’s nice weather today.
        

**Notes:**

- Periods are rarely used in abbreviations in French compared to English. For example:
    
    - M. Dupont → Mr. Dupont
        
    - Dr Martin → Dr Martin
        

---

## **2. Comma (,)**

- Used to separate items in a list, clauses, or for clarity.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - J’ai acheté des pommes, des poires, et des bananes. → I bought apples, pears, and bananas.
        

**Notes:**

- Unlike English, the comma **is not always required before “et”**.
    
- Use to separate subordinate clauses:
    
    - Quand il pleut, je reste à la maison. → When it rains, I stay at home.
        

---

## **3. Semicolon (;)**

- Connects closely related independent clauses or items in a complex list.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - J’aime le thé; mon frère préfère le café. → I like tea; my brother prefers coffee.
        

---

## **4. Colon (:)**

- Used to introduce explanations, lists, quotations, or direct speech.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - Il a dit: « Je viendrai demain. » → He said: “I will come tomorrow.”
        
    - Les ingrédients sont: farine, sucre, et beurre. → The ingredients are: flour, sugar, and butter.
        

**Note:**

- In French, there is a **non-breaking space** before the colon: `:`
    

---

## **5. Question Mark (?)**

- Placed at the end of a question.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - Comment ça va ? → How are you?
        
- **Note:**
    
    - French punctuation requires a **non-breaking space** before `?`
        

---

## **6. Exclamation Mark (!)**

- Expresses strong feelings, commands, or emphasis.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - Attention ! → Watch out!
        
    - Quelle surprise ! → What a surprise!
        

**Note:**

- Non-breaking space before `!`
    

---

## **7. Quotation Marks**

### **a) Guillemets (« ») – French style**

- Used for quotations and dialogue.
    
- **Structure:**
    
    `« Bonjour ! » dit Marie.`
    
    → “Hello!” said Marie.
    

**Notes:**

- A **non-breaking space** is placed **inside** the guillemets: `« Bonjour »`
    
- Can include nested quotations using English-style quotes:
    
    - « Il a dit “je suis fatigué” hier. »
        

---

### **b) English-style quotes (“ ”)**

- Increasingly used in informal writing, but formal French prefers « »
    

---

## **8. Apostrophe (’)**

- Indicates elision, replacing a vowel when two words meet.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - l’homme → the man (instead of _le homme_)
        
    - j’aime → I love (instead of _je aime_)
        

---

## **9. Hyphen (-)**

### **a) Compound words / numbers**

- Used to join words or numbers.
    
- **Examples:**
    
    - un arc-en-ciel → a rainbow
        
    - vingt-et-un → twenty-one
        

### **b) Imperative forms with pronouns**

- Verb + pronoun connected by hyphen:
    
    - Donne-le-moi ! → Give it to me!
        

---

## **10. Ellipsis (…)**

- Indicates omitted text, hesitation, or unfinished thoughts.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - Je ne sais pas… → I don’t know…
        

**Notes:**

- Non-breaking space before the ellipsis in French typography.
    

---

## **11. Parentheses (())**

- Used for extra information, clarifications, or asides.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - Il est parti à Paris (la capitale) pour ses études. → He went to Paris (the capital) for his studies.
        

---

## **12. Dash / Tiret (—)**

- Marks interruptions, appositions, or emphasis.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - Je voulais lui dire — mais je n’ai pas osé. → I wanted to tell him — but I didn’t dare.
        

**Note:**

- French uses **long dash** (tiret) with **spaces before and after**: `—`
    

---

## **13. Slash (/)**

- Used to indicate alternatives or fractions.
    
- **Example:**
    
    - et/ou → and/or
        
    - 1/2 → one-half
        

---

## **14. Accents and Punctuation**

- Accents (é, è, à, û, etc.) must **always be used** in French; they affect meaning:
    
    - ou → or
        
    - où → where
        

---

### ✅ **Summary of Key Differences vs English**

1. **Non-breaking spaces** before `! ? : ;`
    
2. **Guillemets (« »)** preferred over English quotes for formal writing.
    
3. **Hyphenation** for imperative pronouns and compound numbers/words.
    
4. **Ellipsis spacing** differs: … not … (no extra spaces outside French typographic standards).
    
5. Accents are **mandatory**; omitting them can change meaning.
