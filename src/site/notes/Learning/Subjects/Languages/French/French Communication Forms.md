## **1. Politeness Structures**

Politeness is key in French, especially in professional or formal settings.

### **Common Polite Phrases**

|French|Pronunciation|English|
|---|---|---|
|S’il vous plaît|/sil vu plɛ/|Please (formal)|
|S’il te plaît|/sil tə plɛ/|Please (informal)|
|Merci|/mɛʁ.si/|Thank you|
|Merci beaucoup|/mɛʁ.si bo.ku/|Thank you very much|
|Je vous en prie|/ʒə vu zɑ̃ pʁi/|You’re welcome (formal)|
|Excusez-moi|/ɛk.sky.ze mwa/|Excuse me (formal)|
|Pardon|/paʁ.dɔ̃/|Sorry / Excuse me|

**Tips:**

- Use **vous** for formal or unknown people; **tu** for friends/family.
    
- Add **“Monsieur / Madame / Mademoiselle”** for extra politeness:
    
    - Bonjour, **Monsieur**. → Good morning, Sir.

## **2. Formal Letters**

French formal letters follow a **structured format**:

### **Typical Structure**

1. **Header** (sender and recipient info, date)
    
2. **Salutation**
    
    - Monsieur / Madame / Madame, Monsieur (if unsure of gender)
        
    - Avoid “Cher” unless semi-formal.
        
3. **Opening phrase**
    
    - Je me permets de vous écrire pour… → I am writing to you to…
        
    - Suite à votre courrier… → Following your letter…
        
4. **Body**
    
    - Present facts politely, using formal verbs and conditional for requests:
        
        - Je vous serais reconnaissant(e) de… → I would be grateful if you…
            
5. **Closing phrase**
    
    - Je vous prie d’agréer, Monsieur/Madame, l’expression de mes salutations distinguées. → Yours sincerely (formal).
        

**Example:**

`Monsieur Dupont, Je me permets de vous écrire pour vous demander des informations sur... Je vous serais reconnaissant de bien vouloir me répondre. Je vous prie d’agréer, Monsieur, l’expression de mes salutations distinguées.`

## **3. Informal Speech Patterns**

Informal French (friends, family) uses:

- **Tu** instead of **vous**
    
- Contractions and dropping of **ne** in negation:
    
    - Je **n’ai pas** → J’**ai pas**
        
- Common conversational fillers:
    
    - Ben… → Well…
        
    - Alors… → So… / Then…
        
    - Voilà → There you go / That’s it
        

**Examples:**

- Tu viens ce soir ? → Are you coming tonight?
    
- J’ai pas compris. → I didn’t understand.

## **4. Idioms & Common Phrases**

French has many idiomatic expressions—often literal translations sound odd in English:

|French Idiom|Literal Translation|Meaning in English|
|---|---|---|
|Coûter les yeux de la tête|Cost the eyes from the head|Very expensive|
|Avoir le cafard|To have the cockroach|To feel down / depressed|
|Tomber dans les pommes|To fall into apples|To faint|
|Être sur son 31|To be on one’s 31|To be dressed elegantly|
|Donner sa langue au chat|Give one’s tongue to the cat|To give up (guessing)|

**Tip:**

- Learning idioms is crucial for sounding natural.
    
- Many are used in spoken, informal contexts.

## **5. Slang Basics: Verlan**

Verlan is a **form of French slang** where syllables of words are inverted.

**Examples:**

|Standard|Verlan|English meaning|
|---|---|---|
|Femme|Meuf|Woman / girl|
|Lourd|Relou|Annoying|
|Flic|Keuf|Cop / police|
|Bizarre|Zarbi|Weird|
|Argent|Gentar|Money|

**Notes:**

- Verlan is mostly **spoken**, casual, and often used by youth.
    
- Some words get shortened further after repeated use (e.g., meuf stays “meuf”).
    

**Tips for Using Slang:**

- Only use in informal conversations.
    
- Avoid in work, school, or formal letters.
    
- Listen and repeat phrases from movies or songs to get natural intonation.
    

---

### ✅ **Tips for Mastery**

1. **Match register to context**: formal letters → formal structures; texting/friends → informal.
    
2. **Use idioms sparingly** until you are comfortable—they add style but can confuse beginners.
    
3. **Politeness first**: always start formal conversations with Bonjour/Monsieur/Madame and proper titles.
    
4. **Listen to French media** to get a feel for slang (Verlan) and natural informal expressions.
